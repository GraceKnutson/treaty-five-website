About The Numbered Treaties

The Crown and First Nations signed a total of 11 treaties known as the Numbered Treaties between 1871 and 1921, which cover the area between the Lake of the Woods (northern Ontario, southern Manitoba) to the Rocky Mountains (northeastern British Columbia and interior Plains of Alberta) to the Beaufort Sea (north of Yukon and the Northwest Territories). The agreements gave the Crown territory for white colonization and industrial development. Government negotiators made several pledges to First Nations in exchange for their traditional territory, both orally and in the treaty documents. These include exclusive access to treaty-assigned areas as well as the distribution of monetary compensation, equipment for fishing and hunting, and other necessities of farming. These agreement's terms are disputed and contentious to this day. The Numbered Treaties continue to influence Indigenous communities in terms of the law and the economy. Indigenous cultures, rituals, and traditional ways of life have been impacted by the establishment of reserves, schools, and other instruments of assimilation. Moreover, there are persistent disagreements about the verbal and written provisions of the treaties relating to land usage, fishing and hunting rights, and natural resource management. Modern land claims have been sparked by these circumstances. The federal government created a policy recognizing comprehensive and specific claims in 1973 to allay worries regarding treaty fulfilment. The Numbered Treaties have assisted in directing the relationship between the federal government and First Nation peoples by establishing a context of shared obligations and rights, despite the many shortcomings in the treaty-making process. As modern land claims are resolved and concluded, the Canadian government and treaty First Nations work together towards improving the lives of Indigenous peoples.

Information about Treaty 5:

Communities Involved:

  • First Nations tribes located in the northern regions of Saskatchewan, Manitoba, and Ontario
  • Saulteaux tribes
  • Swampy Cree tribes including the number of bands that inhabited the Beren's River region and the Norway House region

Provisions:

  • 160 acres for farming per family of 5
  • The right to hunt, fish and trap
  • Annuities paid annually, $5 per person
  • Chiefs and headmen provided with clothing every 3 years
  • $500 per head and farm stock, tools, equipment and flags presented once
  • Numerous tools, seed and animals for every family and band cultivating on a reserve

Historical Documents Involved:

  • Treaty No. 5 Text
  • Adhesion by Saulteaux or Chippewa Indians
  • Adhesion by Saulteaux or Chippewa and Cree Indians
  • Adhesion to Treaty 5 by Split Lake and Nelson House
  • Adhesion to treaty no. 5 - 1908 - Norway House, Cross Lake, and Fisher River
